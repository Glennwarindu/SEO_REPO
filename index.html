Understanding Self-Efficacy: The Psychology of Belief and Success

By Glenn Warindu | Published on October 13, 2025

Self-efficacy is a psychological concept that refers to an individual's belief in their ability to succeed in specific situations or accomplish a task. It was originally proposed by psychologist Albert Bandura in 1977 and has since been studied extensively in various fields, including health, education, and agriculture.

Self-efficacy affects every area of human endeavor and is a strong predictor of human accomplishment and personal well-being. It influences the choices people make, the amount of effort they put into pursuing their goals, and their resilience in the face of challenges and setbacks. People with high self-efficacy are more likely to view difficult tasks as opportunities to learn and grow, while those with low self-efficacy are more likely to avoid challenging situations and give up more easily when faced with obstacles.

There are several sources of self-efficacy, including mastery experiences, vicarious experiences, verbal persuasion, and emotional arousal. Mastery experiences, or personal successes, are the most effective way to build self-efficacy. Seeing someone else succeed at a task can also boost one's own self-efficacy, especially if the person is perceived as similar to oneself. Verbal persuasion, or encouragement from others, can also be a powerful source of self-efficacy, as can managing one's emotional and physiological responses to challenging situations.

Self-efficacy is an important concept in the workplace, as it is positively and strongly related to work-related performance. Managers can support the development of self-efficacy in their employees by providing accurate descriptions of tasks, clear instructions, and the necessary resources and training. They can also consider self-efficacy when making decisions about developmental or training programs.

Self-efficacy is also an important predictor of health behaviors, including smoking cessation, physical exercise, and diet. People with high self-efficacy are more likely to engage in healthy behaviors and to persist in these behaviors even in the face of challenges. They are also more likely to set higher goals for themselves and to have a more positive outlook on their ability to achieve these goals.

In education, self-efficacy is a strong predictor of academic achievement and persistence. Students with high self-efficacy are more likely to take on challenging tasks, to put in extra effort, and to persist in the face of setbacks. They are also more likely to have a growth mindset, or the belief that their abilities can be developed through effort and learning.

In conclusion, self-efficacy is a powerful psychological concept that influences human behavior and well-being in many domains. It is shaped by a variety of factors, including personal experiences of success and failure, observation of others, verbal persuasion, and emotional and physiological responses to challenging situations. By understanding and fostering self-efficacy, individuals and organizations can support the development of human potential and promote success and well-being.
